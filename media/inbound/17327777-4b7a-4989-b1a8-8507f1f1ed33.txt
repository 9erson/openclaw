The Shekinah—a Hebrew term meaning “that which dwells”—represents God’s tangible presence1, and its connection to the entrance procession reveals how Catholic liturgy preserves Jewish theology about encountering the divine.

In the Temple, a cloud symbolizing the Shekinah filled the sacred space when the ark was placed there1, establishing a visual language for God’s arrival among His people. At Mass, the incense used during the entrance procession functions as an image of this same Shekinah—the cloud of glory descending into the holy of holies—sanctifying the assembled community2. The rising smoke becomes a sensory experience of divine presence entering the worship space, just as Israel witnessed God’s glory cloud filling the Temple courts.

This connection deepens when considering how the Shekinah’s meaning evolved. In the intertestamental period, Jewish tradition held that the Shekinah dwelt in synagogues and among assemblies of the faithful1, suggesting that God’s presence wasn’t confined to the Temple building but accompanied the gathered community itself. The New Testament extends this further, implying that the Shekinah became incarnate in Jesus and later in the Church1. When the priest and ministers process down the aisle, therefore, they’re not simply reenacting Israel’s Temple approach—they’re embodying a transformed understanding where Christ’s body (both the Eucharist and the assembled faithful) becomes the new dwelling place of God’s glory.

The incense specifically recalls the pillar of cloud that guided Israel through the wilderness, symbolizing God’s glory and presence while suggesting both divine transcendence and mystery3. By incorporating this Jewish imagery into the entrance rite, the Church signals that the Shekinah—God’s glorious presence—continues to dwell among His people in a new covenant form.
